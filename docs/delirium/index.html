Delirium

Delirium is a sudden change in brain function — not dementia, not a psychiatric illness — but a medical emergency that signals acute stress on the brain.

What is delirium?

Delirium is an acute failure of attention and awareness. It develops over hours to days, fluctuates during the day, and reflects an underlying medical cause affecting the brain.

Hours–Days
speed of onset

Delirium develops rapidly, unlike dementia which evolves slowly over months to years.

Fluctuates
through the day

Patients may seem clear in the morning and severely confused later the same day.

How common is delirium in the hospital?

15–25%
general medical inpatients

Delirium is common on general hospital floors, especially in medically complex patients.

Up to 80%
ICU patients

Critical illness, mechanical ventilation, and sedation dramatically increase delirium risk.

Why delirium matters

Longer hospital stay
Loss of independence
Increased mortality

Delirium is not just a temporary confusion — it strongly predicts worse medical, functional, and cognitive outcomes.

Common causes

Infection
Dehydration
Electrolyte imbalance
Hypoxia
Medications
Withdrawal
Pain
Urinary retention
Constipation

Delirium is usually caused by multiple problems at once, not just one factor.

Delirium vs dementia

Delirium

Acute onset, fluctuates, caused by illness, potentially reversible.

Dementia

Chronic, slowly progressive, caused by neurodegeneration.

Delirium on dementia

The highest-risk group — delirium often unmasks previously silent dementia.

How delirium is treated

Fix the cause
not just the behavior

The most important treatment is identifying and correcting the underlying medical problem stressing the brain.

Treat infection
Restore oxygen
Correct electrolytes
Stop deliriogenic meds
Normalize sleep
Early mobility
Vision & hearing aids

Does delirium leave lasting effects?

Many patients recover fully, but delirium is also associated with accelerated cognitive decline, especially in older adults and those with pre-existing brain vulnerability.

Full recovery
Persistent cognitive symptoms
Accelerated dementia trajectory

What families can do

Reorient often
Bring glasses & hearing aids
Daylight exposure
Familiar voices
Encourage mobility
Promote sleep at night

Families are one of the most powerful tools we have for preventing and shortening delirium.

Delirium always reflects an underlying medical cause. Any new acute confusion should be treated as an emergency and discussed immediately with the medical team.
