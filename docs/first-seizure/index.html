First Seizure & New Epilepsy

A first seizure is frightening — but not always the start of epilepsy. This page gives a fast, visual overview of what the numbers mean and how to respond safely.

How common are seizures and epilepsy?

~10%
will have a seizure at some point

A single seizure can happen to many people over a lifetime. Most will never develop epilepsy.

1 in 26
will develop epilepsy

Epilepsy means a tendency for recurrent, unprovoked seizures — not just one event.

What’s the chance of another seizure?

After a first unprovoked seizure, the chance of having another one is often quoted around 30–50%. Your individual risk depends on EEG findings, MRI results, seizure type, and other factors.

How treatable is epilepsy?

≈70%
become seizure-controlled on medication

With the right diagnosis and treatment plan, many people with epilepsy live full, active lives — driving, working, studying, and exercising.

Goal: no seizures
Goal: minimal side effects
Goal: protect safety & independence

How long do people stay on medicine?

If someone is seizure-free for several years, their team may discuss whether and when to taper medication. This is highly individualized and depends on seizure type, EEG, MRI findings, and life circumstances (driving, pregnancy, work, etc.).

First seizure

Initial evaluation, safety counseling, and decisions about starting medicine.

First 1–2 years

Adjust dose, monitor side effects, update driving and activity guidance.

2–5+ years seizure-free

For some people, this is when tapering may be discussed. For others, longer treatment is safest.
