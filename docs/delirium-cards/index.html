Delirium

A sudden change in how the brain is working. It is common, serious, and usually treatable.

What is delirium?

Delirium is acute brain stress. It comes on over hours to days, not months or years. It signals that something in the body is upsetting the brain.

How does it look?

Common signs:

  • Trouble paying attention
  • Confused or mixed-up thinking
  • Big change from their usual self

These can come and go during the day.

Up and down during the day

Delirium often fluctuates:

  • Better in the morning, worse at night
  • Moments of clarity mixed with severe confusion

This “up and down” pattern is typical.

Not the same as dementia

Delirium is not dementia:

  • Delirium: sudden, fast change
  • Dementia: slow change over months or years

A person can have both delirium and dementia.

There is always a cause

Delirium is not “just old age” or “just stress.” There is always a medical cause, often more than one:

  • Infection
  • Low oxygen
  • Dehydration
  • Pain or constipation
  • Medicines or withdrawal

How is delirium treated?

The main treatment is to:

  1. Find all the causes
  2. Treat those causes
  3. Support the brain while it heals

Calming medicines alone do not fix delirium.

Step 1: Find the causes

The team may:

  • Check blood, urine, and oxygen levels
  • Review all medicines
  • Look for infection or organ problems

The goal is to find everything stressing the brain.

Step 2: Support the brain

To help the brain recover, we try to:

  • Keep days bright and nights dark and quiet
  • Protect sleep as much as possible
  • Reorient often: who, where, and what day it is
  • Encourage sitting up, movement, and therapy
  • Use glasses and hearing aids

Step 3: When medicines help

Calming medicines may be used only when needed:

  • If the patient may hurt themselves or others
  • If confusion makes vital treatments impossible

We aim for the lowest dose, for the shortest time.

Recovery: first days to weeks

Once causes are treated:

  • Many people improve over days to weeks
  • Confusion usually gets a bit better each day
  • Nights and evenings may stay worse for a while

Recovery: weeks to months

Over the next weeks to months:

  • Some return fully to their usual selves
  • Some improve but not all the way back
  • A smaller group have lasting changes in thinking

Follow-up after the hospital is important.

Delirium and dementia

Dementia and delirium are closely linked:

  • People with dementia are more likely to get delirium
  • People without dementia can still get delirium
  • Delirium can unmask dementia that was not obvious before

Why baseline history matters

It helps the team to know:

  • How was memory and thinking before this illness?
  • Were there already problems with daily tasks or driving?
  • Has there been slow change over months or years?

This guides both hospital care and follow-up.

How families can help

Families are powerful in delirium care:

  • Bring and use glasses and hearing aids
  • Speak calmly with familiar voices and routines
  • Help with orientation: time, place, and reason for being here
  • Encourage eating, drinking, and safe movement with staff

Key takeaway

Delirium is:

  • Acute – comes on quickly
  • Serious – always has a cause
  • Often reversible – but recovery can take time

You can ask the team:
“What is causing the delirium, and how can we help support the brain while it heals?”
